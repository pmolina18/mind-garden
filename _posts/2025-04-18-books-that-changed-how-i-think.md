## Reading as a Superpower

I used to read for entertainment. Now I read for leverage — seeking ideas that compound over years, not just hours.

Here are five books that fundamentally shifted my mental models.

## 1. Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman

The core idea: your brain has two systems. System 1 is fast, intuitive, and often wrong. System 2 is slow, deliberate, and lazy.

**How it changed me**: I now pause before important decisions and ask "Is this System 1 talking?" Usually, it is.

## 2. The Design of Everyday Things — Don Norman

Good design is invisible. Bad design blames the user.

**How it changed me**: I stopped saying "the user is stupid" and started asking "where did *we* fail to communicate?"

## 3. Antifragile — Nassim Taleb

Some things benefit from disorder. The opposite of fragile isn't robust — it's antifragile.

**How it changed me**: I now design systems (and life choices) that gain from volatility rather than just surviving it.

## 4. A Philosophy of Software Design — John Ousterhout

Complexity is the root of all evil in software. Deep modules beat shallow ones.

**How it changed me**: I obsess over interfaces now. A good interface hides complexity; a bad one exposes it.

## 5. Sapiens — Yuval Noah Harari

Humans dominate because we can cooperate flexibly in large numbers through shared fictions (money, nations, religions).

**How it changed me**: I see "obvious" truths differently now. Most of what we take as natural is actually a collective agreement.

## The Common Thread

All five books share something: they reveal that **your default perception of reality is incomplete**. The map is not the territory, and the better your maps, the better your decisions.

What books changed how *you* think?
